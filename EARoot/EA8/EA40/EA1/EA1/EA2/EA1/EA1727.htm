Examples<br/>LinkedIn<br/>Distinctive group: professionals and decision-makers<br/>Other side: recruiters, B2B advertisers<br/>Result: LinkedIn can charge thousands for recruiter seat licences and premium ads because the audience is exactly what they want.<br/><br/>Twitch<br/>Distinctive group: active, engaged gamers and gaming enthusiasts<br/>Other side: game publishers, gaming brands<br/>Result: ad rates and sponsorship deals are much higher than a generic YouTube channel.<br/><br/>4. The key “Steve Blank” takeaway for channels in Phase 1<br/>When you design your channel plan for a multi-sided market, you’re not just thinking:<br/>“How do I get people on both sides?”<br/>You’re also thinking:<br/>“Can I make one side so attractive and distinctive that the other side will pay a premium to reach them?”<br/>This means your early customer acquisition for one side should be laser-targeted to build that value.<br/><br/>1. The starting point: “unsold inventory”<br/>In online advertising, every webpage, app, or platform has slots where ads can go.<br/>If they can’t sell all those slots directly to advertisers, they have unsold inventory.<br/>Ad networks (like Google Display Network) bundle this inventory across hundreds or thousands of sites and sell it cheaply in bulk.<br/>Example:<br/>A small teen blog might not have direct ad sales, so it gives its space to an ad network. That network might bundle it with lots of other teen-oriented sites and sell the package to an advertiser.<br/><br/>2. “Aggregate dozens or hundreds of sites appealing to a specific group”<br/>Advertisers often want a demographic or psychographic segment — e.g., “teenagers interested in gaming.”<br/>But finding enough of them in one place is hard, so ad networks aggregate many small sites to create scale.<br/><br/>3. “The more distinct the audience, and the harder it is to aggregate, the more valuable it will be”<br/>If your platform naturally gathers a highly specific, hard-to-find audience, advertisers will pay more because:<br/>They don’t need to hunt for them across hundreds of random sites.<br/>They know your platform is the place where these people hang out.<br/>Example:<br/>Generic audience: “teenagers” — easy to find across many sites.<br/>Distinct audience: “teenagers who are competitive Fortnite players ranked in top 10%” — much harder to find, but if you have them concentrated in one platform, game brands and esports sponsors will pay premium rates.<br/><br/>4. Why Steve Blank brings this up in Channels<br/>He’s reminding founders:<br/>If your user side in a multi-sided market is distinctive and concentrated, the advertiser side is more valuable.<br/>This shapes your channel plan: instead of chasing generic traffic, you may spend more effort finding and attracting this specific, hard-to-aggregate group — because it makes your other side (advertisers, sponsors) easier to sell to and at higher prices.<br/><br/><br/>
