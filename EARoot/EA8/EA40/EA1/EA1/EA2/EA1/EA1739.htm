<ul>
<li>If a single company has 74% of the market, the market has become an effective monopoly. For a startup, that's an unassailable position for a head-on assault. For instance Google for search or Facebook for social networks.</li><li>If the combined market share for market leader and the second ranking company is greater than 74 % and the first company is within 1..7 times the share of the second, it means a duopoly commands the market. Its position is impervious to attach by a startup. In the telecom sector, Cisco's and Juniper's combined share of the core router market fits this description.</li><li>If a company has 41% market share and at least 1.7 times the market share of the next-largest company, its the market leader.</li><li>For start-up such scenarios with market leader dominance is very difficult market to enter. Market with a clear market leader offers an opportunity for re-segmentation.</li><li>If the biggest player has less than 26% market share, it has no real influence on the market. Startups that want to enter an existing market find these the easiest to penetrate.</li><li>If you decide to attack a market that has just one dominant player, you need to be prepared to spend three times the combined sales and marketing budget of that dominant player. Ouch- so much for going head-on with google or facebook. </li><li>In a market that has multiple participants, the cost of entry is lower, but you still need to spend 1.7 times the combined sales and marketing budget of company you plan to attack. </li><li>To enter an existing market, you must steal market share from an incumbent, hence the war analogy. </li></ul><p/>
