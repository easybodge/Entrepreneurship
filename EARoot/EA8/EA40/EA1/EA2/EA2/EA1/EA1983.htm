The Acquire plan is short, to-the-point, and tactical in nature. The plan helps you find tools that predictably deliver large numbers of “good” customers (who engage with or spend heavily on the site or app) into the widest point of the funnel (at the left) at the lowest cost per customer.<br/><br/>The Acquire plan (see sample plan on page 308) should fit on a single page. It details the first set of Acquire activities to test and includes: <br/>Who: Who’s responsible for driving the program; <br/>who will add support or expertise <br/>What: Describe the tactic and its parts (see below for a list of tools) <br/>Budget: Estimate of spending for the first round of testing <br/>Timing: Outline the steps needed to launch (our example in Figure 9.6 uses a four-week cycle) <br/>Why: Specific, measurable acquisition goals of each plan element Multi-sided or not:<br/>Do you have users, or users and payers?<br/><br/>The acquire plan and tactics will change repeatedly… they’re basically a series of experiments. (see attached).<br/>In the attached spreadsheet, <br/>Who: Two staffers are needed, one full-time and one half-time <br/>What: The tactics, chosen by the team, seem best-suited to maximizing leads and sales (see next section for a list of tools) <br/>Budget: The startup has about $25,000 to spend for this <br/>Timing: The team is allowing itself four weeks to prepare all elements for the launch of validation <br/>Why: The goal is to get 30,000 to 35,000 customers to download a free trial and to get 15 percent of them to convert to paying customers. This would achieve a customer acquisition cost of about $5, as suggested in this plan’s revenue model hypothesis <br/>Multi-sided or not?: Does your product have both users and payers? If so, a multi-sided market requires a separate plan for each “side” of its market. Users will be acquired one way, while reaching and selling advertisers will demand a totally different approach. (Our sample is a single-sided market)<br/>
