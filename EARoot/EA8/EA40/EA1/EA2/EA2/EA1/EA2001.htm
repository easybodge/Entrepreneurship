A classic startup flaw is hiring a sales force before you’ve figured out how to sell.<br/>Your goal is to determine who your true customers are and how they’ll buy your product. You build a sales team only when you completely understand the process that transforms a prospect into a purchaser and know you can sell the product at a price that supports your business model.<br/>The roadmap’s complexity depends on the customer’s size, buying cycle and budget as well as the product’s price, the industry, and the distribution channel selected. The roadmap for selling the CEO of a midsize company is clearer, say, than the route to selling a Fortune 500 VP And roadmaps to Safeway, Intel or Toys “R” Us are far tougher than those leading to local florists or pet stores. Good roadmaps are hard work, but they’re the difference between success and failure. Refine the roadmap while the company is lean and small, before dozens of sales reps find themselves lost on the road, selling without a map.<br/>Three other key sales-planning tools support the sales roadmap: company organization and influence maps, and customer access maps.<br/><br/><br/>
