IPO is not the “end” but a financing event:<br/><ul>
<li>Going public is just another fundraising round, except the investors are the public markets instead of VCs.</li></ul>
<ul>
<li>The company still needs to grow, deliver returns, and be accountable.</li></ul>
<br/>Why IPO?<br/><br/><ul>
<li>Access to a much larger pool of capital than private markets.</li></ul>
<ul>
<li>Liquidity for existing shareholders (though usually with lock-up periods).</li></ul>
<ul>
<li>Branding and credibility boost with customers, partners, and talent.</li></ul>
<br/>VC perspective:<br/><br/><ul>
<li>IPO is one of the main “liquidity events” to return money to their limited partners (LPs).</li></ul>
<ul>
<li>VCs don’t always sell immediately — many are subject to lock-ups too.</li></ul>
<br/>Eligibility / readiness factors:<br/><br/><ul>
<li>Requires strong governance and financial reporting discipline (must operate like a public company well before listing).</li></ul>
<ul>
<li>Market appetite matters — not just company readiness.</li></ul>
<ul>
<li>You must be able to “tell your story” convincingly to public investors (clear growth trajectory, profitability path, competitive moat).</li></ul>
<br/>The IPO process itself:<br/><br/><ul>
<li>Hire underwriters (investment banks) to manage pricing, book-building, and marketing.</li></ul>
<br/><ul>
<li>Draft S-1 registration statement with SEC (detailed disclosures on business, financials, risk factors).</li></ul>
<ul>
<li>Do a “roadshow” — management pitches to institutional investors.</li></ul>
<ul>
<li>Set final pricing and allocation of shares.</li></ul>
<br/>Lock-up periods:<br/><br/><ul>
<li>Typically 180 days when insiders/VCs cannot sell.</li></ul>
<ul>
<li>Designed to prevent a flood of shares from crashing the price.</li></ul>
<br/>Valuation dynamics:<br/><br/><ul>
<li>The IPO price is not set by VCs or founders but by investment banks + market demand.</li></ul>
<ul>
<li>Companies often “leave money on the table” (price below true demand) to ensure first-day pop and build goodwill with investors.</li></ul>
<ul>
<li>This can frustrate founders/VCs but is common practice.</li></ul>
<br/>Alternatives to traditional IPO:<br/><br/><ul>
<li>Direct listings (skip underwriters, no capital raised, just provide liquidity).</li></ul>
<ul>
<li>Mergers with special purpose acquisition companies (SPACs).</li></ul>
<ul>
<li>Each has trade-offs in terms of cost, control, and market signaling.</li></ul>
<br/>After IPO – life changes:<br/><br/><ul>
<li>Heavy compliance: quarterly reporting, Sarbanes-Oxley, SEC scrutiny.</li></ul>
<ul>
<li>Management time shifts from building product to managing investors/analysts.</li></ul>
<ul>
<li>Stock price becomes a daily report card — volatility is inevitable.</li></ul>
<br/>For founders:<br/><br/><ul>
<li>IPO should be pursued only when it aligns with long-term strategy, not just as an “exit.”</li></ul>
<ul>
<li>It’s about building a durable, independent company with public market support.</li></ul><p/>
