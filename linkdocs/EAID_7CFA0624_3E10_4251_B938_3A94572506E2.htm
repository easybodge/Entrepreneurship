Below is a budget-focused outline for achieving roughly 1 km range at 5G sub-6 GHz frequencies (e.g., around 3.3–3.8 GHz). Because this band is less common in consumer Wi-Fi gear than 2.4 GHz or 5 GHz, “budget” components are harder to find. Nonetheless, there are some LTE/CBRS/WiMAX-focused products that can work well for short to medium distances.

Important:

The 3.3–3.8 GHz range often falls under licensed or lightly licensed regimes (like CBRS in the U.S.). Always check local regulations for allowable frequencies and power levels.

2. Budget Power Amplifier (3.3–3.8 GHz)
Why It’s Needed
The USRP B210’s internal transmit power (~+10 dBm typical) may not be sufficient for 1 km unless you have perfect line-of-sight and a high-gain antenna.
A small/medium power amplifier (e.g., 1–5 W) in the 3.3–3.8 GHz band can help ensure a reliable link if your environment has obstacles or interference.




Rationale:

Boosting from ~10 dBm to, say, +30 dBm (1 W) or more can significantly improve your link budget.
Ensure the amp’s linearity is acceptable if you’re using wideband 5G-like waveforms (high PAPR).







3. Low-Noise Amplifier (3.3–3.8 GHz)
Why It’s Needed
Improves receive sensitivity by lowering overall noise figure.
If you have obstacles, low signal, or want robust two-way comms, an LNA near the antenna is often helpful.
Especially relevant if you’re running a long coax from antenna to the SDR, or if you have an environment with high interference.



Rationale:

If your link is borderline or you want to maintain better data rates, an LNA can improve the SNR on the receive side.
Not always mandatory for a 1 km link, but highly recommended if you also have a power amp on TX (to balance the link budget).

4. Band-Pass Filter (3.3–3.8 GHz)
Why It’s Needed
Even a budget power amp might produce out-of-band emissions.
Regulatory rules for the 3.5 GHz band (CBRS) can be strict about spurious emissions.
A band-pass filter ensures you stay within your allocated band, reduce spurious signals, and help mitigate strong out-of-band interferers.







Rationale:

Ensures cleaner output from your amplifier (reducing harmful out-of-band transmissions).
Helps protect your receiver from strong signals outside your 3.5 GHz band.

Final Notes and Considerations
Heat Dissipation: High-power amplifiers will need heat sinks and possibly forced air cooling.
Power Supply Requirements: PAs often need 12 V, 24 V, or 28 V supplies at several amps.
Regulatory Compliance:
Check local laws (FCC in the US, ETSI in the EU, etc.).
You must ensure that your total EIRP (PA power + antenna gain) does not exceed legal limits.
Linearity and PAPR: 5G signals have high Peak-to-Average Power Ratios, so choose an amplifier capable of linear operation at your required output power.
Cables and Connectors: At these frequencies, cable losses can be substantial. Use low-loss coax (e.g., LMR-400, LMR-600) and maintain good connector quality.
Availability: Part numbers may change or have lead times, especially for custom filters/duplexers. Always confirm stock and lead time with your distributor.
