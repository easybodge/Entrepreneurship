�� How Companies Get into a Magic Quadrant
Gartner defines the MQ scope annually for specific markets (e.g. “Cloud AI Developer Services” or “Endpoint Protection Platforms”).

They publish inclusion criteria – e.g., must have:

Minimum revenue or customer base

Product available globally

Product must meet core functional requirements

Public customer references

Call for Vendors:
Gartner may reach out to known vendors or open up for applications or nominations.

You submit data:

Product details

Customer references

Roadmaps

Revenue and strategy

Competitive positioning

Analysts conduct interviews with your team and your customers.

Evaluation and scoring:
Gartner uses its internal criteria to place vendors in the quadrant (not shared externally in detail).

❗Important Notes
You must be invited or qualify for inclusion. You cannot pay your way into a quadrant.

If your product doesn’t yet meet the inclusion threshold, you may:

Be considered for other Gartner research (like "Market Guide" or Cool Vendor reports)

Build a relationship with Gartner analysts via briefings (free), or consulting (paid)

�� So How Do Others Use It?
Buyers (CIOs, procurement leads) use MQs to:

Shortlist vendors (e.g. only look at “Leaders”)

Justify vendor selection internally

Benchmark vendor strengths/weaknesses

Vendors use MQ placement as:

A marketing badge (“Leader in Gartner MQ”)

Credibility in sales decks and investor pitches

Strategic feedback from Gartner analysts

�� If You’re a New Vendor
Here’s what you can do instead:

Book a free Gartner briefing (not an evaluation, but a way to introduce your company to analysts).

Build traction, customer base, and product capabilities to meet inclusion criteria.

Monitor the MQ reports for your space, and when Gartner opens the next cycle, submit to be considered.

Use Market Guide or Cool Vendor reports as stepping stones — these often highlight earlier-stage or emerging players.
